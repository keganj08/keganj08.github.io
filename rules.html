Blank White Cards

Official Rules

Overview

1000 Blank White Cards is a card game where the players create the cards as part of the game. The only objective is to get the most points.

Key Terms

  • Discard- To "discard" a card is to liut a card from your hand into the discard liile.
  • Ghost - A "ghost" is a player who is currently dead.
  • Random - A action determined "randomly" must be decided with the use of a coin or die.
  • Reveal - To "reveal" a card to a player is to show them the card for an ample amount of time.
  • Opponent - An "opponent" is an entity on the scoreboard that is not you nor your minion.
  • Round - A "round" consits of each player taking one turn.
  • Target - The "target" of a card is the entity on whom the card is played.
  • Turn - Each player gets their own "turn"; There are many turns in a round.
  • User -The "user" of a card is the one playing it.

Cards

Structure

Every card has a picture, a title, and a description of its effect. Some cards also have a label such as "Player Effect" when appropriate.

Targets

There are two types of cards: Target Cards and Neutral Cards.

Target Cards are cards that specify an action that must be carried out (Ex: “Fall Out of a Treehouse, Lose 50 pts”). Minion Cards also fall under this category. You may play these cards on Yourself or An Opponent. Some cards specify that they must be played on a certain target- always follow the instructions on the card. If a card requires you to flip a coin or roll a die before its effect takes place, you must choose a target before determining the result.

Neutral Cards are cards that either affect everyone equally (Ex: “All Batteries Fire, Fire! All players lose 150 pts”) or do not require any choice (Ex: “Red Shell, The next player misses their next turn and loses 50 pts”). These cards are not played on any one person, but rather played to the board. Some cards preclude the targeting of a specific player (Ex: “Forced Treaty: You and the user may not play cards on each other.”); neutral cards that affect this player may still be played.

To target a player’s minion you must be able to target its owner. All minions are a sub-target of their owner. If a minion has Taunt, they must be targeted instead of the corresponding player or any of their other minions.

Card Types

Cards are categorized as either Point, Effect, Reaction, Minion, or Special.

Point Cards have either a positive or negative point value. When they’re played, the player being targeted gains or loses that many points. (Ex: “You Get the Mustang For the Night, +150 pts”)

Effect Cards introduce a new effect to the game. Some are temporary, while others are permanent. These effects can grant points on every turn, change an aspect of gameplay, and more. These cards are further categorized as either Player Effects or Board Effects. Player effects impact only one player, whereas board effects can impact all players. (Ex: “Off Your Phone! The next player to go on their phone loses 1000 pts”)

Reaction Cards are played immediately after a certain condition is met, usually when another player plays a card, instead of on your turn. (Ex: “Oh, I Don’t Think So. Cancel a card played by another player”)

Minion Cards are like effect cards, but can only be removed by lowering its health to 0. Each minion has a starting health and a maximum health. Players can remove health by making the minion lose points and give health by making it gain points. A minion’s health cannot exceed its maximum health and is discarded once its health reaches 0. Unless specified otherwise, a minion performs its effect on your turn, every turn. (Ex: “Sunflower: Each turn, draw a card. Health: 50, Max Health: 150”)

Special Cards are any cards that don’t fall into any of these categories. They usually allow actions such as drawing cards or cancelling another player’s action. (Ex: “Card Magnet, Draw 2 cards”)

Tiers

The cards are divided into 4 tiers: Common, Rare, Epic, and Legendary. The tiers are simply a way to identify a card’s general value or ability to impact the game. You can tell a card’s tier by the color of the circle in the top right corner.

Common Cards are represented by the color green. They tend to be point cards with values ranging from 50-150 or relatively insignificant effect cards.

Rare Cards are represented by the color orange. They tend to be point cards with values ranging from 150-250 or cards that offer some strategic potential.

Epic Cards are represented by the color blue. They tend to have significant point values, effects, or other abilities that make them very useful.

Legendary Cards are represented by the color purple. They have the potential to make a large impact on the game through very high point values, powerful effects, or rare abilities.

Non-Deck Cards

Some cards are not in the deck, but rather influence the game in another way. There are two types of such cards: Ages and Heroes.

Ages are permanent board effects that cannot be removed using regular methods. If playing with Ages, all Ages are shuffled in a separate deck at the beginning of the game with the top card face-up. When an age is face-up, its effect applies to everyone in the game (Ex: “April 16th, 2000: All coin flip / die roll effects are doubled”). To “advance an age” means discard the top card of the Ages deck and flip the next card face-up. To “regress an age” means flip the top card face-down and put the most recently discarded age on top.

Heroes are permanent player effects that cannot be removed using regular methods. If playing with Heroes, each player draws a random Hero at the beginning of the game and only they may use its effect. Some cards let you draw a new hero; in this case, randomly draw from the Heroes deck before returning your old Hero. Some cards give you a special Hero that cannot be obtained via other means (Ex: “Curse of the Amulet: Swap your Hero with Hamulet”). These Heroes are not included in the Heroes deck and are kept in a separate pile.

How To Play

Getting Started

A dealer will start by dealing everyone around 7 cards. The number of starting cards can be changed based on the number of players. Next the player to the left of the dealer starts their turn. Once their turn is done, play continues clockwise.

Taking Your Turn

You always start your turn by drawing a card from the deck. Then you choose a card from your hand to play. You must play a card each turn. When you play a card, you place it face-up in the discard pile next to the deck. The only exception to this is for Effect cards, which are left out until their effect is finished, or Minion cards, which are left out until defeated. Once a card has been placed it must be played and can not be taken back for any reason. Unless otherwise specified, you may only play one card per turn.

If you have a card in your hand that says “Must play immediately,” play it on your turn after you draw a card. Unless the card says otherwise, these can only be played on yourself. If you have multiple “Must play immediately” cards, you may choose which one to play. Any player who is caught holding onto a “Must play immediately” is penalized 500 points. If the same player does this twice in one game they are eliminated.

Certain cards, such as “Reaction” cards, may be played any time in response to another player’s card- even when it isn’t your turn.

If you have no cards that may be played, you must discard a card instead. This is the only time you may choose to discard a card.

Death

Some cards have effects that “kill” a player; from this point forward, this player is a Ghost. Upon turning into a ghost, the player must discard all active player effects. While a ghost, the player’s score cannot change, they cannot use their minions, they cannot gain player effects, and most importantly, they cannot win the game.

On their turn a ghost may play a card normally or they may Haunt. A ghost can haunt an opponent by discarding a card to make them lose 50 points. When a player is revived they are no longer a ghost and all regular rules once again apply.

If all players are ghosts at the end of the game, the ghost with the highest score wins.

Endgame

Once there are no more cards left in the deck, everyone takes one last turn where no cards are drawn (including the player who drew the final card.) Anything that would have happened after this turn, such as a player missing their next turn, gets cut off. After everyone has taken their last turn, the player with the most points wins!

Team Variant

1000 Blank White Cards may be played in teams! Each team must have an equal number of players, but there is no limit on the number of teams nor on the number of players comprising a team. Each team’s members must be staggered equally in the turn order. For example, if there are teams A, B, and C, each with members 1 and 2, the turn order must go A1-B1-C1-A2-B2-C2. There are two main rule changes. First, each team gets one combined score rather than individual player scores. Note that this means no two teammates are considered opponents. Second, after each completed round, each player passes a card to the closest team member to their left. Whichever team has the highest score at the end of the game wins.

Creating New Cards

Every card must have a picture (you don’t have to be an artist!) and a description of its effect. Titles are also recommended. Beyond this, it is strongly encouraged that you follow a few guidelines to help your card fit into the game better.

  • Certain types of cards must have special markings on them to make their purpose clear. These include “Nope” Cards, “Deflect” Cards, and “Must play immediately” Cards. Refer to existing cards for examples of what markings to include on your card.
  • Cards should not have excessively powerful effects, such as “You win the game” cards. These cards are generally not very fun to play with and go against the spirit of the game.
  • Overly complicated cards can be too confusing to play with. Try to limit your cards to one main effect that can be easily explained in a few sentences.

Have fun!
